Scientific Definition of Bioplastics

According to Plackett (2011) and Kumari et al. (2023), Bioplastics are polymeric materials that are either:

  1. Bio-based (derived partially or fully from renewable biological resources),
  2. Biodegradable,
  3. Or both.
From a polymer chemistry perspective (Stevens, 1999; Koltzenburg et al., 2017):
Bioplastics are macromolecules composed of repeating monomeric units obtained from biomass such as starch, cellulose, vegetable oils, or microbial fermentation products, rather than petrochemical feedstocks.

But not to forget, bio-based does not automatically mean biodegradable, and biodegradability depends strongly on polymer structure and environmental conditions.

Example of Bioplastics

Polylactic Acid (PLA) with the chemical name of Poly(lactic acid), repeating unit derived from lactic acid (2-hydroxypropanoic acid), which comes under the chemical classification of aliphatic polyester.


How to obtain:

According to Plackett (2011) and Ali et al. (2023):

  1. To have glucose, we have to hydrolyze corn or sugarcane
  2. Glucose undergoes microbial fermentation to produce lactic acid
  3. Two lactic acid molecules undergo a condensation reaction involving dehydration (water removal), followed by cyclization, to form a cyclic dimer known as lactide.
  4. Then, lactide undergoes ring-opening polymerization, which we obtain PLA. This is a polymerization reaction.

What makes bioplastics chemically different?

Using Stevens (1999) and Koltzenburg (2017):
Bioplastics differ structurally from petroleum plastics because:
  • They often contain ester linkages
  • They contain oxygen-rich backbones
  • They are less chemically inert
  • They are more susceptible to hydrolysis and enzymatic attack
In contrast, petroleum plastics like polyethylene have nonpolar C–C backbones that resist degradation.

Life cycle of bioplastics

1Lower fossil fuel dependenceYes
2Reduced greenhouse gas emissions (in some cases)Yes
3Higher agricultural land useNo
4Energy-intensive fermentation and purification processesNo

Therefore: The environmental advantage of bioplastics depends on feedstock source, production efficiency, and waste management infrastructure.

Scientific Definition of Petroleum-Based Plastics

Petroleum-based plastics are synthetic polymers derived from fossil fuel feedstocks, primarily crude oil and natural gas.

According to Stevens (1999) and Koltzenburg (2017):
Petroleum-based plastics are macromolecules formed by polymerization of small hydrocarbon monomers obtained from petrochemical refining processes.
Kumari et al. (2023) further classify them as conventional plastics produced via large-scale industrial addition polymerization.

Example of Petroleum-Based Plastics

Polyethylene (PE): With the chemical name of Poly(ethylene), repeating unit derived from ethylene (ethene, C₂H₄), which comes under the chemical classification of addition polymer (polyolefin).

How to obtain

According to Stevens (1999), Koltzenburg et al. (2017), and Kumari et al. (2023):

  1. Crude oil or natural gas is extracted from underground reservoirs.
  2. Crude oil undergoes fractional distillation, where one of the important fractions obtained is naphtha.
  3. • Naphtha undergoes steam cracking, which breaks large hydrocarbons into smaller molecules such as ethylene (C₂H₄).
  4. Ethylene undergoes addition polymerization under high pressure and in the presence of catalysts, forming long chains of Poly(ethylene) (PE). This is a polymerization reaction.

What makes petroleum-based plastics chemically different?

Using Stevens (1999) and Koltzenburg et al. (2017):

Petroleum-based plastics differ structurally from bioplastics because:

  • They contain nonpolar carbon-carbon (C–C) backbones
  • They lack oxygen or nitrogen atoms in their main polymer chains
  • They are highly chemically inert and hydrophobic (water-repelling)
  • They are resistant to hydrolysis and enzymatic attack due to their stable, saturated hydrocarbon structure

In contrast, bioplastics like PLA contain ester linkages and oxygen-rich backbones that are more susceptible to hydrolysis and enzymatic breakdown. This fundamental difference in molecular structure explains why conventional plastics persist in the environment for centuries while some bioplastics can biodegrade under the right conditions.

Life cycle of petroleum-based plastics

1High fossil fuel dependenceYes
2Significant greenhouse gas emissions during productionYes
3Requires crude oil extraction and refiningYes
4Energy-intensive distillation and cracking processesYes
5Non-renewable resource consumptionYes
4Persists in environment for centuries (minimal degradation)No

Therefore: Petroleum-based plastics have a well-established, efficient manufacturing infrastructure, but their life cycle is linear from finite fossil fuels to persistent waste creating significant environmental challenges at both the production and end-of-life stages.

Impacts of Plastics in Today's World

Using Kumari et al. (2023), Osman et al. (2023), and Ali et al. (2023):

  1. Environmental Persistence

    2. Petroleum plastics have stable C–C backbones that resist degradation, persisting for centuries (Kumari et al., 2023).

  2. Microplastic Pollution

    3. These plastics fragment into microplastics smaller than 5mm through weathering. Osman et al. (2023) explain that microplastics leach toxic additives and adsorb environmental pollutants, posing risks to ecosystems and human health.

  3. Carbon Footprint

    4. Petroleum plastic production requires energy-intensive fossil fuel extraction and refining, contributing significantly to greenhouse gas emissions (Kumari et al., 2023).

  4. Bioplastic Trade-Offs

    5. Ali et al. (2023) show through Life Cycle Assessment that bioplastics reduce fossil fuel dependence but may increase agricultural land use, water consumption, and fertilizer pollution.

  5. Bio-based ≠ Biodegradable

    6. Kumari et al. (2023) emphasize that bio-polyethylene is chemically identical to petroleum-based PE same C–C backbone, same persistence. True biodegradability requires hydrolysable bonds like ester linkages (Plackett, 2011).

Therefore: A plastic's environmental impact depends on its molecular structure and full life cycle, not just its origin.

Conclusion

However, in this project, I learned a lot, but as a conclusion of what I made, I can say:
I explored the chemical differences between bioplastics and petroleum-based plastics using six scientific sources.

What I learned is that the key difference lies at the molecular level. Petroleum-based plastics like polyethylene have stable carbon-carbon backbones that make them durable but also cause them to persist in the environment for centuries. Bioplastics like PLA contain oxygen-rich backbones with ester linkages, which make them more susceptible to hydrolysis and enzymatic breakdown.

However, my research also taught me that the story is not simple. Bio-based does not automatically mean biodegradable. Some bioplastics are chemically identical to petroleum plastics and will persist just as long. Additionally, while bioplastics reduce fossil fuel dependence, they come with their own trade-offs like agricultural land use and water consumption.

The environmental impact of any plastic depends on its molecular structure and its entire life cycle—from how it is made to what happens after it is used.
